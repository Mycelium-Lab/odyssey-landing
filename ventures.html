The discipline

Built for economics that actually work

A product can look busy and still lose money on every single user. Before we pour fuel on a venture, the maths has to work — quietly, on a spreadsheet, at small scale.

We watch the relationship between what it costs to earn a customer and what that customer is worth over time. If acquisition can't pay for itself inside a window we can actually fund, the venture doesn't graduate — no matter how good the demo looks.

That discipline is unglamorous, and it's exactly why the ones that survive keep surviving.

Where we play

What makes a market worth entering

Before we commit a team, a market has to clear four tests — and most don't.

01

Proven demand

People are already searching, paying, or hacking together workarounds. We look for a problem the market has confessed to — not one we have to convince it exists.

02

Winnable competition

Incumbents exist but are slow, expensive, or badly designed. Competition proves the market; a beatable field is where a small, fast team can take share.

03

Clear monetization

A believable path to revenue from day one — subscription, freemium, or in-app — with unit economics that work before we spend a euro scaling them.

04

Real retention

The problem recurs, so the product earns a place in someone's week. Retention is the only growth that compounds — we won't build something people use once.

How we find them

The signals we watch

Ideas don't come from brainstorms. They come from watching a handful of signals across our verticals — and moving when several line up at once.

01

Demand you can measure

Rising search volume, waitlists, and people paying for clumsy workarounds. Real intent always leaves a trail — we just have to read it.

02

Pricing gaps

Incumbents that are overpriced, over-complicated, or bundling things people never asked for. Every gap they leave is an opening for us.

03

Shifting behaviour

New platforms, regulations, or habits that quietly change what people expect — and make yesterday's market leader suddenly beatable.

04

Underserved segments

Big, specific groups the current players ignore because they look "too small to matter." In our verticals, they rarely turn out to be.
